What I learned from lesson 4 in MIL that Sir Lee Mar Y. Sabugar taught is about the media information sources. Media information sources, specifically, are about searching for information. These sources are where we get our news and information. They include newspapers, TV, online news sites, social media, and other platforms that share information with the public. There are three main sources of information: indigenous knowledge, libraries, and the modern tool to gather information on the internet. Indigenous knowledge pertains to the distinct customary knowledge systems and practices that are cultivated, amassed, and transmitted throughout successive generations within particular cultural and ethnic communities. A library is an intentionally chosen assortment of well-arranged information resources usually kept in a physical or virtual location, made available to the general public for reading, research, and borrowing. The internet is a global network of electronically connected computers and gadgets that facilitates communication and information sharing. Users can access a wide range of resources via it, such as web pages, online services, and multimedia files. Overall, we learned that the understanding of media information sources becomes evident when, for instance, we are seeking information about a current event. Suppose there is a news story circulating on social media regarding a local community issue. In this scenario, we can observe the use of both traditional and modern media information sources. We might first turn to a reputable online news site to get detailed and verified information about the event. Simultaneously, we could consult social media platforms to gauge public reactions and perspectives on the matter. Additionally, let's consider a situation where an individual is interested in learning about sustainable gardening practices. They may tap into indigenous knowledge by consulting elders or community members who possess traditional wisdom about eco-friendly farming methods. To complement this, they might visit a local library to borrow books and resources on sustainable agriculture. Furthermore, the internet can serve as a valuable tool for accessing online forums, articles, and videos that share contemporary insights into environmentally conscious gardening practices.

What i learned from lesson 5 from MIL that sir Lee Mar Y. Sabugar discussed.Language is the primary means of communication and comprehension in a variety of settings, including the media. It functions as a technical and symbolic code. Symbolic codes are essential for communicating meaning to the audience through signs and symbols in the setting of media language. While the connotative meaning allows for a range of audience interpretations, the denotative meaning is the media text's literal interpretation. Understanding the pre-established meanings and structures inside a particular media product is necessary in order to interpret media texts correctly. This is where media codes and conventions come in. Narrative frameworks that help an audience understand the meaning of media communications are included in media language. The way a media text is understood as a whole depends on the usage of locations, mise en scene, acting, color, and other factors. The language of media is further enhanced by technical codes unique to each type of media, such as those pertaining to lighting, soundtrack, camerawork, and editing. A formal written language is also provided via written codes, such as speech bubbles, titles, captions, and headlines, in media products. Conventions provide the methods for applying these norms and support the organized interpretation of media content. They can range from form conventions to plot and genre conventions. Producers in the media are the language architects; they use conventions and norms to craft messages for the target audience. Media content's meaning and narrative are shaped by the interaction of technical and symbolic codes as well as convention compliance. Deciphering these languages as media consumers enables us to interact with and evaluate the messages that media creators convey, emphasizing the complex interrelationship between language, communication, and the potent influence of media on our lives.
-Zion Canta
